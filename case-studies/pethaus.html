03 The Sitch

The brief was e-commerce.
The problem was anxiety.

Competing platforms were built for browsing. But 83% of participants arrived with a specific product already in mind — they needed to check, not shop, and that gap was the entire opportunity.

The Anxious Health Advocate

"I check every ingredient before I'll even consider buying."

Dog with a newly diagnosed beef allergy. Anxiety isn't about price — it's about accidentally causing a flare-up. One unreadable label ends the session.

The Overwhelmed First-Timer

"I don't know what's good — every recommendation feels like a guess."

First-time kitten owner. No way to know what's good or safe. Every vague recommendation is a reason to close the tab. Needs proof and guidance before committing financially. Anxiety is about wasting money on things a young pet won't need.

Two completely different anxieties — one rooted in medical history, one in inexperience — converging on the same behaviour. Neither could act without feeling certain first. That's not a content problem or a filter problem. It's a structural one.

04 The Design — Move 01

Pet first.
Always.

Core IA Shift 01 / 04

Before any browsing, Paw Haus asks: who are you shopping for?

Enter your pet's name, species, age, and health conditions. Every product page then checks itself against that profile — surfacing what's safe, flagging what to watch. Shopping becomes checking.

Placement Strategy 02 / 04

Trust Signals Above the Fold

Not buried in footers. Labelled based on measured assurance and tailored dietary considerations. Trust has to be earned before the click — not asked for after it.

At a Glance Ingredients

Top ingredients shown as chips — colour-coded green, amber, or red against your pet's specific health profile. No hunting. No collapsed accordions. Instant legibility at the point of decision.

08 Reflection

What working alone
taught me a team couldn't.

01

You Design Bolder When You Own the Risk

Without a team to water down decisions, I had to commit fully. The pet-first onboarding departed from every e-commerce convention. Owning that bet solo meant it either landed or didn't. It landed.

02

Research Is Strategy, Not Just Input

Running the full research myself meant insights were alive in my head when I designed. The 'At a Glance' module didn't come from a brief — it came from watching someone squint at a collapsed accordion for 40 seconds and give up.

03

One Strong Metric Beats Five Weak Ones

I could have chased task completion, click-throughs, time-on-page. Instead I designed for one thing: "Do you feel confident right now?" That focus made every decision clearer and every test more honest.

04

Trust Is a Design Ingredient, Not a Feature

Trust isn't a badge you add at the end. It's baked into where information appears, when it appears, and what it looks like. That's a systems problem, not a visual one.

09 What's Next

If I Had 3 More Months

What I'd Build Next.

Subscription & Auto-Replenishment

Pet owners reorder the same food. A subscription model with dosage reminders would reduce decision fatigue and build habit-based loyalty.

Vet Integration Layer

75% relied on vet validation before any new product. A direct vet-recommendation integration would close the loop between clinical advice and purchase decisions.

Ingredient Transparency API

Partner with sourcing databases to auto-populate ingredient origin data — not just "locally sourced" as a badge, but traceable to the farm or facility.
