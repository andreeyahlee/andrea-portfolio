3 of 7 issues survived every design iteration — because they were business-model problems. No UI change fixes anonymity trust, manager authority, or cadence assumptions.

Managers could see problems they couldn't fix

The authority to restructure workloads sits with HR — not with the managers ARVO was built for. The platform prescribed solutions its users couldn't execute.

08 The Outcome

Four issues survived.
Three were the
client's to solve.

Presenting this to the client was the hardest facilitation moment of the project. The framing had to be precise: this isn't a design failure. It's clarity — you now know exactly where design ends and where product strategy begins.

A designer who papers over business-logic failures with prettier interfaces isn't solving problems. They're delaying them. I wrote formal strategic recommendations for each of the three business issues and presented them as the client's next product decisions.

Partially Addressed

AI Jargon

AI output was too academic and jargon-heavy. ARVO team must simplify all prompts to be clear and professional for B2B users.

Business Decision

Anonymity gap

Workers may not answer honestly if they fear being identified, leading to managerial scepticism. ARVO team needs to decide on a min. anonymity threshold to guarantee results cannot be traced back to specific team members.

Business Decision

Digital Homework — Survey Cadence & Managerial checklist

Weekly surveys and checklists cause burnout and feel like "just another task." ARVO should switch to a monthly or fortnightly schedule.

Business Decision

Structural resignation

Managers feel helpless when alerted to systemic issues (e.g., pay, promotion slots) they have no authority to fix.

10 Retrospective

What leading this
actually taught me.

Hold the design boundary — out loud

Knowing where design ends isn't enough. The skill is explaining that boundary to a client who doesn't want to hear it, in a way that feels like partnership rather than refusal. That takes preparation, not just conviction.

Workshop design is leadership design

How you structure a co-creation session shapes what a client is willing to accept afterwards. Opening with user quotes before any findings was a deliberate facilitation choice — it changed the entire tone of what followed.

Scope decisions are the hardest decisions

Fixing three things well beats fixing seven things partially. Defining what we were not doing in the iteration sprint — and holding that boundary with the team — was harder than the design work itself.
