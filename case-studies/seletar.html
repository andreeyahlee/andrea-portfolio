02 The problem

The airport's best feature
was invisible online.

Seletar Airport saves passengers 45–90 minutes over KLIA. But the existing website couldn't communicate this. It read like a corporate brochure — no flight info, no practical planning tools, no reason to return.

We were briefed to redesign it. Research told us the real brief was bigger: prove the competitive advantage, or passengers would keep going to KLIA by default.

03 What we found

We talked to 9 passengers. Every single one said the same thing.

9

Interviews — 8 Firefly passengers, 1 private jet traveller

100%

chose Seletar for one reason: it saves them time

0

of 9 had ever used the official website to plan their trip

"Seletar's product is speed and ease. Anything that creates confusion or uncertainty breaks that promise."

The insight that shaped every decision that followed

This reframed the entire project. We weren't solving a design problem — we were solving a communication problem. The airport's biggest advantage was invisible to the people most likely to use it.

Ranked problems

Critical

The website feels irrelevant (8 of 9)

It reads like a company brochure, not a travel planning tool. No one trusted it.

High

Getting to accommodation is stressful (7 of 9)

30–45 min Grab waits, 1 bus route, no exit guidance. Smooth experience ends at the doors.

Medium

First-timers feel unprepared (4 of 9)

Weather delays and unfamiliar aircraft types created anxiety with no reassurance online.

Each ranked problem became a direct question for the team to design against.

01 Task-first navigation

SEQ SCORE 4.3 → 4.8

FIX

Users arrived with a specific job — check a flight, find transport, know what to bring. The old navigation was organised around the airport's structure, not the passenger's task.

Our A/B test revealed "Check Status" confused users, who mistook it for booking confirmation. We pivoted to "View Flight Schedule." Cognitive friction disappeared instantly — SEQ jumped to 4.8/5.

05 Turning research into design  —  2 / 3

In V2, the module defaulted to Public Bus — wrong for most users who take taxis.

02 A dedicated "Getting Home" section

→ Improvement: Single-page architecture

FIX

My Round 1 finding named it "arrival blindness" — the entire transport section was written for people going to the airport, not people who just landed. The team redesigned it to surface one-tap options for taxis, Grab, Gojek, and buses on arrival.

Round 2 surfaced a follow-up: the module defaulted to Public Bus — wrong for the majority. V3 fix: taxi surfaced first.

05 Turning research into design  —  3 / 3

03 Cut turboprop education entirely

FIX

We had designed a whole section explaining the aircraft. My testing showed users didn't want it. One person engaged with it — out of personal aviation curiosity, not travel anxiety. Everyone else skipped it.

I recommended cutting the section and redirecting that space to food, facilities, and journey steps. The team did. That's what research influence looks like — and that's how we dealt with the operational anxiety passengers had, especially first-timers at Seletar Airport.

We ran two rounds of testing.

08 Retrospective

Research is only useful
if it changes something.

01

The bravest research output is a cut

I recommended removing a whole designed section — turboprop education — because the data was clear. That recommendation was accepted. Research influence means being willing to say "this didn't work" out loud.

02

A single word can be a research finding

"Check Status" vs "View Flight Schedule" — the difference between a confused user and a confident one. I found this by watching people hesitate. SEQ went from 4.3 to 4.8 on that single change.

03

Research earns its value when it sharpens the brief

My most important output wasn't a report — it was the reframe: Seletar's product is speed. Design to protect it. Every decision that followed was easier because of that one clear principle.

04

Round 2 always finds what Round 1 couldn't

Tourist payment confusion was invisible until we tested our second design iteration. You can't anticipate every edge case from a brief — but you can catch it before it ships.

What I'd test next

Transport default state

Does surfacing Grab/Taxi first actually reduce friction for arriving passengers? I'd run a preference test to confirm the right ordering.

Tourist payment copy

I identified the EZ-Link confusion. I'd test rewritten copy — global payment methods first, local second — with international users specifically.

Food findability

Users looked for dining and couldn't find it under Passenger Guide. A tree test would confirm where it belongs in the navigation.
